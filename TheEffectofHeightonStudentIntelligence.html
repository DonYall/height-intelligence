There will be a linear positive correlation between height and academic performance. As people get taller, they gain intelligence and ultimately perform better in their high school courses.

Data Analysis

Note that height is always the x-axis.

It is clear that the relationship between height and academic excellence is a downward, polynomial trend with degree 4. The effective mathematical functions for each course is given below, labeled A(h), C(h), and E(h), where:

h is the student’s height, in centimeters,

A is the student’s performance in Advanced Functions, as a percentage,

C is the student’s performance in Calculus and Vectors, as a percentage,

E is the student’s performance in English, as a percentage.

A(h) = -0.0009759949668h4 + 0.6940558348h3 - 184.8361549h2 + 21847.25h - 966912.2327

C(h) = -0.001239244072h4 + 0.8802469896h3 - 234.1667556h2 + 27650.4272h - 1222685.931

E(h) = -0.0009286198166h4 + 0.6512799593h3 - 171.0967232h2 + 19954.64081h - 871656.42

It is noticeable that each of these functions have negative coefficients, which represent their end behaviour. Since the degree of the function was found to be even, lower values of h result in a lower performance, but higher values of h also result in a lower performance.

However, these functions should not be misunderstood as they are derived from a small domain of values from 164cm - 190cm. Therefore, extreme assumptions such as “A high school student 155cm tall would have an English average of lower than 0” cannot be justified. However, the assumption can be made that there is indeed a correlation between height and intelligence due to the similar nature in all 3 individual graphs. They are combined and shown in the graph below. Their respective opacities are equal to their R2 values.

Each function, within the domain of heights collected, includes two points which are local maximums, and one point which is a local minimum. These points were found to be very similar for Calculus, Advanced Functions, and English. These critical points can be found by taking the derivative of each function. The results are shown below.

Students with heights closer to the maximum values in the table above perform better in their studies, and students with heights closer to the minimum value perform worse in their studies.

However, in all functions, the first local maximum point was found to be the absolute maximum. This means that there is a slow negative correlation between a student’s height and their academic performance. The steepness of these correlations can be expressed by forcing linear trendlines between height and performance. A graphic representation of this is shown below.

All three trendlines point downwards, but their slopes are very gradual. This is because they are rough and inaccurate estimates compared to the quartic graphs. With all above information, three mathematical conclusions can be drawn:

As h increases, A(h), C(h), and E(h) all decrease.

limh->165.8 and limh->187.4 of A(h), C(h), E(h) are maximums.

limh->177.4 of A(h), C(h), and E(h) is a minimum.

Conclusion

The collected data did not agree with the initial hypothesis. In fact, it turned out to be quite the opposite. A student’s academic performance, which directly correlates to their intelligence, decreases as their height increases, however, the height of minimum intelligence seems to be around 177.4cm.

One possible explanation for the overall decreasing trend could be that students, especially those who approach adulthood in their teens, care much about their height. They care so much that they would do things like exaggerate it to others, take notes of it, and stand in a dominating position to those who are shorter. Essentially, as their height increases, so does their ego. As their ego increases, so does their academic confidence. As their academic confidence increases, their actual performance decreases because they believe they have the power to do anything since they are tall.

This would also explain the height of minimum intelligence, 177.4cm. There would always be a certain height where individuals are neither satisfied with their height nor willing to get taller. This is the stage where they are most egotistical, which, in turn, causes these individuals to give less effort into anything. This claim can be backed up by the fact that most teens in Bayview Secondary School had their emo or TikTok addict phase at around 177.4cm. This is where they would put the least effort into their academic studies, and where their laziness is at a maximum.

Evaluation

This experiment was not perfect. The relations found were accurate to most students, but not all. Two possible sources of error for this investigation are discussed below.

Some students may have been lying about their height. The conclusion for this experiment explained that egotistical students could have been the cause of the minimum values in the graph, however, it is also a source of error. If students around 177.4cm were most likely to lie or exaggerate their height, it is likely that the minimum in the graphs should have been much lower than it actually was. This does not, however, defy the trendline in any way. It simply affects the data collected and the graphs composed. This can be avoided by manually taking the measurements of each student, using the same person and scale to measure.

The sample collected did not control factors such as race, gender, sexual orientation, chess rating, penis length, typing speed, or desired future career. Factors like these likely affected the variation in the graphs, and the trendlines composed could have been completely different functions than the quartic ones discovered. In the future, this could be avoided by taking accurate measurements of these variables for each student manually. Quantitative values, such as penis length, should be measured by the same observer for each student.
